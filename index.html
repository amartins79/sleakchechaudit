The problem

Small websites are the easy target

Most attacks aren't a hacker picking your business by name. They're automated bots scanning thousands of sites a day, looking for the same handful of weaknesses — and small sites rarely have anyone watching.

You don't need to be a big brand to be worth attacking. You just need a gap.

  • Data theft. Customer details, logins and payment info can be quietly skimmed for months.
  • Lost trust. A defaced page or a "this site may be hacked" warning sends customers straight to a competitor.
  • Privacy fines. A data breach can trigger reporting duties — and heavy penalties — under data-protection laws like GDPR and CCPA.

What a finding actually looks like

Here's a sample of the kind of issues we catch — and how we explain them.

62 / 100
Needs attention

Example score for a typical small-business site before fixes.

High

No Content Security Policy (CSP)

Your site doesn't tell browsers which scripts are allowed to run, so an attacker who slips code in could quietly steal data from your visitors. Fix: add a Content-Security-Policy header that restricts scripts to trusted sources.

Medium

Third-party scripts aren't verified (no SRI)

You load scripts from other companies without checking they haven't been tampered with — if one is hijacked, your site loads the hacked version too. Fix: add Subresource Integrity hashes to external scripts.

Medium

Weak HTTPS enforcement (HSTS)

Visitors can be quietly downgraded to an insecure connection, exposing logins and payment details to anyone watching the network. Fix: send a Strict-Transport-Security header with a long max-age.

Medium

Missing Referrer Policy

Your pages may leak private web addresses and customer information to other sites your visitors click through to. Fix: set a Referrer-Policy header such as strict-origin-when-cross-origin.
