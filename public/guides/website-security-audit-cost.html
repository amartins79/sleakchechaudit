What you're actually paying for

Security "audit" covers a wide spectrum, and the price tracks how much human expertise is involved:

  • Free automated scanners — run a URL, get a raw report. Useful for a first look, but no prioritisation, context or plain-English explanation.
  • Focused small-business audits (~$100–$500) — automated checks plus human review, delivered as a readable report with a fix list. This is the sweet spot for most small sites.
  • Manual penetration tests (low thousands and up) — a specialist actively tries to exploit your site. Thorough and appropriate for larger or high-risk applications, but overkill (and unaffordable) for a typical brochure or small e-commerce site.
  • Ongoing monitoring / retainers — recurring fees for continuous scanning and alerts, on top of the initial audit.

What changes the price

  • Automated vs manual. Human testing time is the single biggest cost driver.
  • Scope. A single marketing site is cheaper to assess than a large app with logins, payments and integrations.
  • Depth. A configuration and headers review costs far less than exploit-based testing.
  • Remediation. Some audits stop at findings; others include fix guidance or a re-check afterwards.

Cheap vs expensive: how to choose

The trap at both ends is spending money in the wrong place. A free scan you can't interpret rarely leads to fixes, so the risk stays. A multi-thousand-dollar penetration test is excellent but wasted on a five-page small-business site that's simply missing standard headers. For most small businesses, the highest return comes from a focused, human-reviewed audit that tells you — in plain English — exactly what's wrong and what to fix first.

Is a website security audit worth it?

For most small businesses, yes — the one-time cost is small next to a breach's cost in lost trust, downtime, remediation and potential penalties. An audit turns unknown risk into a clear action list.

What's the difference between a security scan and a penetration test?

A scan is largely automated and flags known issues; a penetration test is a manual, in-depth attempt to exploit weaknesses, done by a specialist at much higher cost. Many small sites are well served by a thorough, human-reviewed scan-based audit.

Are free website security checkers any good?
